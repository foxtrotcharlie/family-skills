# Medical Sources

This file lists the evidence-based sources to draw on when generating a weekly file. The goal is breadth and quality — no single source dominates, and the user explicitly does not want a prescriptive single-protocol approach (see how `vittorio-protocol.md` is now framed: as one input among many, not the spine of the document).

The user wants science-based content. That means: physiological mechanisms over folk wisdom, peer-reviewed or clinically reviewed sources over commercial parenting sites, and "here are the options and the evidence" over "do this".

## How to use this file

1. **No inline citations in the body.** The reader doesn't want a bibliography in the body — they want a warm-friend register that happens to be accurate. Sources inform *your* reasoning; they don't appear inline. The single exception is the static "Sources" footer line at the bottom of every weekly file (see `template.md`) — that names broad source families (NHS, Mayo, ACOG, etc.) and links to the public repo's per-domain provenance files. The body stays footnote-free.
2. **Triangulate.** For any factual claim about fetal development or maternal physiology, the claim should appear in at least two of the Tier 1 / Tier 2 sources below. If only one source mentions it, hedge ("some research suggests…") or skip it.
3. **Prefer mechanism over magic.** When something is happening (nausea, fatigue, breathlessness, weird dreams), the science-backed explanation goes in the detailed breakdown. The brief view can stay surface-level, but the detail layer is where the "why" lives.
4. **Default to options, not prescriptions.** When something is a choice (screening tests, vaccines, birth interventions, supplementation beyond folate/iodine/vit D), present the evidence and the trade-offs. Don't tell them what to do.

## Tier 1 — Authoritative medical bodies

These are the spine of factual claims. When in doubt, anchor here.

### NHS (nhs.uk and nhsinform.scot)
- **Best Start in Life week-by-week**: https://www.nhs.uk/best-start-in-life/pregnancy/week-by-week-guide-to-pregnancy/
- **Ready Steady Baby (NHS Inform Scotland)**: https://www.nhsinform.scot/ready-steady-baby/
- **Strengths**: Clinically reviewed, cautious phrasing, UK/international conventions match the user's voice (mum, booking appointment, midwife). Uses "she may experience X" rather than "you will experience X" — language to borrow.
- **Use for**: Fetal development summaries, what symptoms to expect, when to call midwife/care provider, screening test windows.

### Mayo Clinic (mayoclinic.org)
- **Fetal development 1st/2nd/3rd trimester pages**: https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/pregnancy-week-by-week
- **Strengths**: Concise, well-structured by week, US-context but largely transferable. Good on symptoms with brief mechanism explanations.
- **Use for**: Cross-checking developmental milestones, symptom lists with light physiology.

### Cleveland Clinic (my.clevelandclinic.org)
- **Fetal development overview**: https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/articles/7247-fetal-development-stages-of-growth
- **Strengths**: Clear three-stage framing (germinal/embryonic/fetal), good for the "what stage are we in" header context.
- **Use for**: Developmental stage framing, system-by-system summaries.

### ACOG — American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (acog.org)
- **Patient resources**: https://www.acog.org/womens-health
- **Strengths**: Authoritative on US obstetric practice, screening guidelines, vaccine timing (Tdap, RSV, flu, Covid).
- **Use for**: Confirming clinical recommendations, especially for "decisions" sections in the to-dos.

### RCOG — Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (rcog.org.uk)
- **Patient information leaflets**: https://www.rcog.org.uk/for-the-public/
- **Strengths**: UK clinical authority, often gentler in framing than ACOG, useful for international/British context.
- **Use for**: Cross-referencing UK guidance — relevant given the user's British English preference.

### WHO (who.int)
- **Maternal health**: https://www.who.int/health-topics/maternal-health
- **Strengths**: Global guidance, less US-centric.
- **Use for**: Antenatal care recommendations, vaccine schedules in international context.

## Tier 2 — Evidence-based educators and systematic reviews

These are where to anchor "the evidence on X" — especially for contested or choice-heavy topics.

### Evidence Based Birth (evidencebasedbirth.com)
- **Resources for parents**: https://evidencebasedbirth.com/resources-for-parents/
- **Strengths**: Rebecca Dekker, PhD — translates Cochrane reviews and primary studies into accessible articles. Strong on labour and birth choices specifically.
- **Use for**: The "decisions" portions of to-dos in the third trimester especially. Topics include: due date accuracy, membrane sweeps, induction timing (the ARRIVE study), eating in labour, IV fluids in labour, fetal monitoring, waterbirth, vitamin K, big-baby concerns, GBS management, group B strep, advanced maternal age.
- **Tone note**: Matches the user's preference well — presents evidence, lays out trade-offs, doesn't tell people what to choose.

### Cochrane Library (cochranelibrary.com)
- **Pregnancy and childbirth reviews**: https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/reviews/topics
- **Strengths**: Gold-standard systematic reviews. Dense — usually summarized via Evidence Based Birth or NHS, but go to source for contested questions.
- **Use for**: Verifying claims about interventions when sources disagree.

### StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books)
- **Embryology entries**: peer-reviewed chapters on weekly embryology (e.g., "Embryology, Weeks 1", "Embryology, Weeks 6–8")
- **Strengths**: Peer-reviewed, freely accessible, accurate developmental detail.
- **Use for**: Accuracy checks on early-pregnancy weeks (1–12) where consumer sites sometimes oversimplify or get the timing wrong.

## Tier 3 — Peer-reviewed papers on maternal physiology (the "why am I feeling like this" layer)

The user specifically wants the science behind what mum is experiencing. These three papers cover most of what shows up in the `Mum's body` detailed breakdown.

### Soma-Pillay et al. (2016) — Physiological changes in pregnancy
- **Citation**: Soma-Pillay P, Nelson-Piercy C, Tolppanen H, Mebazaa A. *Cardiovasc J Afr*. 2016;27(2):89–94. DOI: 10.5830/CVJA-2016-021
- **PMC**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4928162/
- **Why this one**: Lead author is at University of Pretoria — locally relevant given the user is in Cape Town. Comprehensive review of cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, haematological, and gastrointestinal changes.
- **Use for**: Understanding why she's breathless (50% increase in cardiac output, diaphragm displaced upward), why she's needing the loo (60–80% increase in renal plasma flow), why she's dizzy on her back (vena cava compression after ~20 weeks), why heartburn (progesterone relaxes the lower oesophageal sphincter).

### Jee & Sawal (2024) — Physiological Changes in Pregnant Women Due to Hormonal Changes
- **Citation**: Jee SB, Sawal A. *Cureus*. 2024 Mar 5;16(3):e55544. DOI: 10.7759/cureus.55544
- **PMC**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10993087/
- **Use for**: Hormonal mechanisms behind GI symptoms, mood, immune adaptations.

### Chiang et al. (2025) — Molecular and Cellular Basis of Physiological Changes in Pregnancy (with neurologic focus)
- **Citation**: Chiang YT, Chen JH, Chen KH. *Int J Mol Sci*. 2025;26(11):5220. DOI: 10.3390/ijms26115220
- **PMC**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12154306/
- **Use for**: Maternal brain changes (matrescence) — gray matter reductions, white matter reorganization, the role of estradiol/progesterone/prolactin/oxytocin in brain remodeling. Useful for the `What to say to her` section: she isn't "going crazy" or "losing her mind" — her brain is actually being remodeled, and there is solid neuroimaging evidence for this. This is reassuring, science-backed framing.

## Tier 4 — Mainstream parent sites (use as inspiration for format, not as primary source)

### BabyCenter (babycenter.com)
- **Week-by-week**: https://www.babycenter.com/pregnancy/week-by-week
- **The user named this site as a useful format reference.** They have a medical advisory board and the per-week pages are well-structured with: size comparison, this-week summary, what's happening with mum.
- **Use for**: Format reference for the size-comparison header and the rough one-week-at-a-time scope. **Don't import their content directly** — cross-check facts against Tier 1 sources.

### What to Expect (whattoexpect.com)
- **Strengths**: Familiar format, broadly accurate.
- **Use for**: Sanity-checking common phrasings, but treat as Tier 4 — verify against Tier 1.

## Topic-specific sources

### Perinatal mental health
- **NHS perinatal mental health**: https://www.nhs.uk/pregnancy/keeping-well/mental-health/
- **Maternal Mental Health Alliance (UK)**: https://maternalmentalhealthalliance.org/
- **Postpartum Support International**: https://www.postpartum.net/
- **Use for**: The `What to say to her` section, antenatal anxiety/depression framing. Antenatal depression is under-discussed — these sources are direct about it without alarmism.

### Nutrition and supplementation
- **NHS pregnancy diet**: https://www.nhs.uk/pregnancy/keeping-well/have-a-healthy-diet/
- **NICE antenatal care guideline (NG201)**: https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng201
- **Use for**: Folic acid, vitamin D, iodine, iron — the genuinely evidence-supported supplements. The user explicitly does not want generic "eat healthy" advice; these sources support specific, evidence-backed recommendations.

### Birth preparation and choices
- **Evidence Based Birth (already listed above)** — primary go-to
- **Spinning Babies** (spinningbabies.com): for fetal positioning techniques in the third trimester. Mainstream-adjacent, not all peer-reviewed, but widely used.
- **AIMS — Association for Improvements in the Maternity Services (UK)**: https://www.aims.org.uk/ — informed-choice oriented, useful counterweight to a paternalistic clinical framing.

### South African / local context
- **Soma-Pillay paper above** is locally authored.
- **South African Department of Health Maternity Care Guidelines**: search for the latest "Guidelines for Maternity Care in South Africa" PDF — useful for understanding the local public-system context if the user signals it matters for a particular week (e.g., when discussing screening test availability or vaccine schedules).

## What NOT to use

- **Generic blog posts and content farms** without medical review (sites like the cloudninecare.com, lighthouseprc, ngc.clinic results that show up in searches — they aggregate broadly correct info but also folk claims).
- **"Mommy blogs"** as primary sources — fine for relatable framing if cross-checked, never as evidence.
- **Pinterest infographics** — they're often wrong on developmental specifics.
- **Anything claiming a single "natural" or "right" way to do pregnancy / birth / postpartum.**
- **Old wives' tales that have been repeatedly debunked**: heartburn doesn't reliably predict hairy babies (Johns Hopkins did look at this and found a weak correlation, but it's not useful), the "baby gender by bump shape" is folklore, raspberry leaf tea evidence is weak and contested, "eating for two" is wrong (the calorie increase is modest and trimester-dependent).

## Quick decision tree for the model

When drafting a week and you're not sure where a claim should come from:

1. **"What size is the baby this week?"** → Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, NHS. Cross-check at least two.
2. **"What developmental milestone happens this week?"** → NHS Best Start, StatPearls for early weeks, Cleveland Clinic for general.
3. **"Why is she feeling X symptom?"** → Soma-Pillay 2016 for cardiovascular/renal/respiratory/GI, Jee & Sawal 2024 for hormonal mechanisms, Chiang 2025 for cognitive/emotional/brain.
4. **"Should they do X intervention/test?"** → Frame as a decision. Evidence Based Birth for the trade-offs, ACOG/RCOG/NICE for standard-of-care framing. Never tell them what to choose.
5. **"What about her mental health?"** → NHS perinatal mental health, MMHA. Direct but not alarmist.
6. **"Is this a red flag?"** → NHS "When to call your midwife" pages, RCOG patient info leaflets.

If a claim doesn't fit any of these and the source is unclear: leave it out, or hedge with "some find that…" rather than asserting it.
