# Exercise During Pregnancy: Evidence-Based Guidelines, Benefits, Risks, and Recommendations

## Executive Summary

Exercise during pregnancy is safe, beneficial, and recommended for most women with uncomplicated pregnancies. All major international guidelines—ACOG, WHO, Canadian (CSEP), Australian, and IOC—converge on a core recommendation of **at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week** [1][2][3][4]. The evidence base, built on multiple large meta-analyses and RCTs, demonstrates meaningful reductions in gestational diabetes (~34% risk reduction), hypertensive disorders, cesarean delivery, excessive weight gain, and perinatal depression [5][6][7]. Concerns about miscarriage, preterm birth, or fetal growth restriction have not been substantiated for women without contraindications [1][9]. Exercise should be individualized and modified as pregnancy progresses, with specific absolute and relative contraindications guiding clinical decisions.

---

## 1. Current Evidence-Based Guidelines

### 1.1 ACOG (United States) — Committee Opinion No. 804, April 2020

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends [1]:

- Women with uncomplicated pregnancies should engage in **aerobic and strength-conditioning exercises** before, during, and after pregnancy.
- Target: at least **20–30 minutes per day on most or all days** of the week (aligning with ≥150 min/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity).
- Women who habitually engaged in vigorous-intensity activity before pregnancy can continue during pregnancy.
- Activity restriction (bed rest) should **not** be routinely prescribed to reduce preterm birth.
- The "talk test" and Borg Perceived Exertion Scale (RPE 13–14, "somewhat hard") are preferred intensity monitors over heart rate.

### 1.2 WHO — Guidelines on Physical Activity and Sedentary Behaviour, 2020

The World Health Organization's first-ever pregnancy-specific guidance recommends [2]:

- **At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity** throughout the week.
- Activity should incorporate a variety of types (aerobic and strengthening).
- Sedentary behaviour should be limited; replacing sedentary time with any intensity of activity yields benefits.

### 1.3 Canadian Guideline (CSEP), 2019

Key recommendations [3]:

- Accumulate **at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity each week** over a minimum of 3 days per week.
- Incorporate both aerobic and resistance training.
- Pelvic floor exercises are recommended.
- "Prenatal physical activity should be considered a front-line therapy for reducing the risk of pregnancy complications."
- Physical activity is **not** associated with miscarriage, stillbirth, neonatal death, preterm birth, preterm/prelabour rupture of membranes, neonatal hypoglycemia, low birth weight, birth defects, induction of labour, or birth complications.

### 1.4 Australian Government

- **2.5–5 hours of moderate-intensity** or **1.25–2.5 hours of vigorous-intensity** physical activity per week [4].
- Include pelvic floor exercises during and after pregnancy.
- Aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities both recommended.

### 1.5 International Olympic Committee (IOC), 2016

- Addresses recreational and elite athletes specifically [14].
- Vigorous-intensity exercise into the third trimester appears safe for healthy pregnancies.
- Avoid supine exercise, high-altitude intense training (>2,500m without acclimatization), and contact/high-impact sports.

## 2. Benefits of Exercise During Pregnancy

### 2.1 Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

The strongest and most consistent benefit is the reduction of GDM risk:

- **Paulsen et al. (2023):** Meta-analysis of 18 RCTs showed exercise vs. no exercise reduced GDM risk — **RR 0.66 (95% CI: 0.50–0.86)** [5]. No subgroup differences by modality, intensity, or supervision.
- **Network meta-analysis (BMC Public Health, 2025):** Group-based physical activity at healthcare facilities was most effective — **RR 0.59 (95% CI: 0.49–0.72)** [11].
- **Large meta-analysis (Nature, 2024):** 116 studies, 40,940 participants confirmed group-based interventions reduce GDM — **RR 0.66 (95% CI: 0.46–0.95)** [12].
- For overweight/obese women: ACOG 804 cites aerobic exercise as significantly reducing GDM incidence [1].

### 2.2 Hypertensive Disorders and Preeclampsia

- ACOG 804 reports aerobic exercise 30–60 min, 2–7 times/week is associated with significantly reduced risk of gestational hypertensive disorders [1].
- **MDPI 2024 meta-analysis** of RCTs confirms physical activity's role in reducing maternal hypertensive disorders [8].
- **Paulsen et al. (2023):** Preeclampsia reduction did not reach significance overall (RR 0.65, 95% CI 0.42–1.03), but subgroup analysis suggests mind-body and low-intensity exercise may be effective [5].

### 2.3 Cesarean and Operative Delivery

- ACOG 804 reports **RR 0.69 (95% CI 0.42–0.82)** for cesarean birth among exercising women [1].
- A 2016 meta-analysis in normal-weight women confirmed significantly higher vaginal delivery rates and lower cesarean rates [1].
- For women with pregestational conditions: prenatal exercise reduced cesarean odds by 55% (OR 0.45; 95% CI 0.22–0.95) [1].

### 2.4 Gestational Weight Gain

- Exercise shows a modest decrease in overall weight gain (1–2 kg) in normal weight, overweight, and obese women [1].
- More importantly, exercise helps prevent *excessive* gestational weight gain.

### 2.5 Preterm Birth

- Exercise is **not associated with increased preterm birth risk** [9].
- In overweight/obese women, aerobic exercise may reduce preterm birth (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.41–0.95) [1].
- A 2023 systematic review (J Clin Med) specifically concluded exercise during pregnancy is not a risk factor for preterm delivery [9].

### 2.6 Birth Weight

- Differences are minimal to none between exercising and non-exercising women [1][10].
- Women who exercise vigorously through the third trimester may deliver infants 200–400g lighter, but without increased risk of fetal growth restriction [1].

### 2.7 Mental Health

- **Wang et al. (2022):** Meta-analysis of 20 RCTs (n=2,866) found exercise significantly reduces perinatal depression symptoms — **OR 0.62 (95% CI: 0.45–0.86)** [6]. Walking produced the largest effect (SMD −1.06). ≥150 min/week and ≥12 weeks duration produced greatest improvements.
- **Davenport et al. (2018, BJSM):** Prenatal exercise reduces both prenatal and postnatal anxiety and depressive symptoms [7].
- Exercise is recommended as a "front-line" non-pharmacological intervention for perinatal depression [6].

### 2.8 Other Benefits

- Reduced low back and pelvic girdle pain [1]
- Reduced urinary incontinence (with pelvic floor exercises) [3]
- Faster postpartum recovery [1]
- Improved/maintained physical fitness and cardiorespiratory capacity [1]

## 3. Recommended Exercise Types and Intensity

### 3.1 Safe Exercises (Extensively Studied per ACOG 804) [1]

| Type | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Walking | Accessible, low-barrier; most effective for depression [6] |
| Stationary cycling | Low-impact, safe throughout pregnancy |
| Swimming/water aerobics | Buoyancy reduces joint load (~83% weight reduction); linked to better perineal outcomes |
| Aerobic dance | Modified for balance changes |
| Resistance training | Weights, elastic bands; builds strength for labor and postpartum |
| Stretching/flexibility | Modified yoga, Pilates (avoid hot versions) |
| Pelvic floor exercises | Recommended by all guidelines [1][3][4] |

### 3.2 Intensity Guidelines

- **Target:** Moderate intensity (RPE 13–14 on Borg scale; "somewhat hard") [1]
- **"Talk test":** Should be able to carry on a conversation while exercising [1]
- **Previously active women:** Can continue vigorous intensity with no demonstrated adverse effects [1]
- **Previously sedentary women:** Should follow gradual progression [1][15]

### 3.3 Duration and Frequency

- Minimum: 150 min/week spread over ≥3 days [1][2][3]
- Ideal: 20–30 min/day on most or all days [1]
- High-intensity/prolonged sessions (>45 min) may cause hypoglycemia; ensure adequate caloric intake [1]

### 3.4 Trimester Considerations

- **First trimester:** Most normal activities can continue; avoid hyperthermia (no hot yoga/saunas); nausea/fatigue may limit activity temporarily [1][13].
- **Second trimester:** Often the most comfortable period; avoid supine positions after 16–20 weeks [1][13].
- **Third trimester:** May need to reduce intensity; modify for balance changes; vigorous exercise appears safe but should be individualized [1][14].

## 4. Contraindications

### 4.1 Absolute Contraindications (Do Not Exercise)

Per ACOG 804 [1] and Canadian 2019 guidelines [3]:

- Hemodynamically significant heart disease
- Restrictive lung disease
- Incompetent cervix or cerclage
- Multiple gestation at risk for premature labor
- Persistent second- or third-trimester bleeding
- Placenta previa after 26–28 weeks of gestation
- Premature labor during the current pregnancy
- Ruptured membranes
- Preeclampsia or pregnancy-induced hypertension
- Severe anemia
- Intrauterine growth restriction [3]
- High-order multiple pregnancy (e.g., triplets) [3]
- Uncontrolled Type I diabetes [3]
- Uncontrolled hypertension [3]
- Uncontrolled thyroid disease [3]

### 4.2 Relative Contraindications (Discuss with Provider)

- Recurrent pregnancy loss [3]
- Gestational hypertension [3]
- History of spontaneous preterm birth [3]
- Mild/moderate cardiovascular or respiratory disease [1][3]
- Symptomatic anemia [1][3]
- Malnutrition or eating disorder [1][3]
- Twin pregnancy after 28th week [3]
- Cardiac arrhythmia not investigated [1]

### 4.3 Warning Signs to Stop Exercising [1]

- Vaginal bleeding
- Regular painful contractions
- Amniotic fluid leakage
- Dyspnea before exertion
- Dizziness or headache
- Chest pain
- Muscle weakness affecting balance
- Calf pain or swelling
- Abdominal pain

## 5. Exercises to Avoid

| Activity | Reason | Source |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Scuba diving | Fetal pulmonary circulation cannot filter bubble formation | [1] |
| Contact sports (football, rugby, hockey, martial arts) | Risk of abdominal trauma | [1][13][16] |
| Activities with high fall risk (horse riding, downhill skiing, gymnastics) | Trauma/impact risk | [13][16] |
| Supine exercise after 16–20 weeks | Aortocaval compression → hypotension | [1][13][14] |
| Hot yoga / hot Pilates / exercising in high heat & humidity | Hyperthermia risk, especially first trimester | [1][14] |
| High-altitude intense exercise (>2,500m without acclimatization) | Limited data on safety; potential fetal hypoxia | [14] |

## 6. Previously Sedentary vs. Active Women

### Previously Active Women
- Can continue pre-pregnancy exercise regimens during pregnancy, including vigorous activity [1].
- May need modifications as pregnancy progresses (balance, supine position, impact) [1].
- Elite athletes should train under close supervision, avoid hyperthermia, maintain hydration and caloric intake, and consider reducing resistance loads [1][14].

### Previously Sedentary Women
- Should begin with low-intensity, short-duration exercise and gradually increase [1][15].
- Goal: progressive adjustment toward 150 min/week of moderate-intensity activity [1][3].
- Even modest activity yields clinically meaningful benefits [3].
- ACOG emphasizes pregnancy as an "ideal time for behavior modification" [1].

## 7. Safety Evidence Summary

| Concern | Evidence | Source |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Miscarriage | NOT associated with exercise; no RCTs reported exercise-related spontaneous abortion | [3][5] |
| Preterm birth | NOT increased; may be reduced in overweight/obese women (RR 0.62) | [1][9] |
| Fetal heart rate | 10–30 bpm increase during/after exercise — normal adaptive response | [1] |
| Core temperature | Rises <1.5°C over 30 min in thermoneutral environment; does not reach teratogenic range | [1] |
| Birth weight | No clinically significant reduction; vigorous 3rd-trimester: 200–400g lighter without growth restriction | [1][10] |
| Bed rest effectiveness | No evidence it prevents preterm birth; causes harms (VTE, deconditioning, bone loss, psychosocial harm) | [1] |

## 8. Evidence Quality and Limitations

### Strengths
- Multiple large meta-analyses with consistent findings across countries
- Major guideline convergence (ACOG, WHO, Canadian, Australian, IOC)
- RCT-level evidence for key outcomes (GDM, hypertension, depression, preterm birth)

### Limitations and Gaps
- Most RCTs study moderate-intensity exercise; limited data on very high intensity (>90% max HR) [1]
- Dose-response upper limits not well-defined
- Limited data on first-trimester vigorous exercise specifically [14]
- Elite athlete data is sparse [1][14]
- Heterogeneity in exercise interventions across studies
- Most studies in high-income countries
- Occupational physical activity effects are mixed and limited [1]

## Open Questions

1. Is there an upper threshold of exercise intensity/duration beyond which fetal risk increases?
2. What is the optimal exercise prescription for elite athletes during pregnancy?
3. Are benefits transferable to low-resource settings with different baseline activity levels?
4. What is the long-term impact of prenatal exercise on offspring metabolic health?
5. Can exercise-based interventions meaningfully reduce preeclampsia risk (current evidence borderline)?
