# "What to say to her" — Mental Health Section Guide

This section was specifically requested and matters to the user. Treat it with care.

## Purpose

Pregnancy is emotionally complex in ways that are often invisible. This section gives the partner specific phrases they can borrow when they don't know what to say, plus principles that help the wording transfer to other moments.

It is NOT a replacement for professional support. It IS a small, practical companion for ordinary hard moments.

## Brief view structure

```markdown
## What to say to her
[1-2 sentence opening that names what she's likely feeling this week]

- "[Phrase]"
- "[Phrase]"
- "[Phrase]"
- "[Phrase]"
- "[Phrase]"
```

Use 4-5 phrases. They should be:
- **Specific to the week's emotional landscape.** Week 8 anxiety about miscarriage is different from week 36 fear of birth.
- **Naturally spoken** — sound like things a real partner would say, not a Hallmark card
- **Validating, not fixing** — "I hear you" energy, not "let me solve this"
- **Honest** — partner can say "I'm scared too" or "I don't know what to say" if that's true


## Trimester-specific emotional landscapes (use as guidance, vary by week)

**First trimester (esp. weeks 6-12):**
- Loneliness of not being able to tell people yet
- Anxiety about miscarriage (especially pre-12-week scan)
- Body feels foreign; nausea/exhaustion makes everything harder
- Ambivalence is common even in wanted pregnancies
- Decision fatigue around testing/screening

**Second trimester (weeks 14-27):**
- Often the "easier" trimester emotionally, but body changes are accelerating
- Anxiety around anomaly scan (~20 weeks)
- First movements bring new bonding but also sometimes anxiety about counting them
- Identity shifts — "becoming a mother" hits in different ways
- Body image challenges as bump becomes visible
- Decisions about birth plan, classes, maternity leave

**Third trimester (weeks 28-40):**
- Anticipation, fear of birth itself
- Sleep deprivation already starting — affects mood
- Discomfort, restricted mobility, feeling huge
- "Nesting" energy alongside anxiety about readiness
- Reduced fetal movement worries
- End-of-pregnancy fatigue and "I just want this baby out"
- Postnatal anxiety often starts before birth

## What "looking after yourself too" means by trimester

- **First:** Partners often feel useless and helpless. Suggest talking to friends who've been through it.
- **Second:** Mid-pregnancy is a good time for partner to have own check-in, talk to therapist if struggling.
- **Third:** Partner postnatal depression risk is real and rarely talked about. Pre-baby therapy/support setup matters.

## Anchored numbers to use (anchored, not inline-cited)

When sharper than folk numbers fits, draw on `perinatal-emotional-journey.md`:

- Perinatal depression: ~12–18% pooled (often "1 in 6"). Antenatal depression alone ~10–15%.
- Perinatal anxiety: ~15% antenatal, ~15% postnatal — often missed by depression-focused screens.
- Partner perinatal depression: ~8–10% pooled, peaking 3–6 months postpartum, correlated with maternal depression.
- Baby blues: 50–80% of postpartum women, days 3–5, resolves spontaneously by ~2 weeks.
- Postpartum psychosis: 1–2 per 1,000 — emergency, see below.
- Maternal brain remodeling (Hoekzema 2017): measurable selective gray-matter changes lasting at least 2 years and predicting bonding strength. Useful for "your brain is literally rewiring" reassurance — biology, not folk wisdom. Belongs in *Mum's body* detailed breakdown, not here.
- Partner emotional support is the strongest modifiable protective factor for maternal perinatal mental health (Pilkington 2015). Useful framing for the partner role: clinically significant, not just supportive.

## The intrusive-thoughts vs psychosis distinction (critical)

Distressing intrusive thoughts of harm to the baby occur in the **majority of new mothers** and are **not predictive of harm**. Distress about the thought is reassuring, not alarming. The thought "what if I dropped the baby" that horrifies the parent is the safe kind.

The opposite — **believed, ego-syntonic thoughts** about the baby (often with severe insomnia, confusion, or grandiosity in the first 2–4 weeks postpartum) — is the emergency. This pattern is postpartum psychosis, not OCD-spectrum intrusive thoughts. The two superficially resemble each other and are clinically opposite.

When postpartum-period content surfaces in a weekly file (later third trimester, postpartum weeks if those become part of the skill), the *Watch for warning signs* and *Red flags* sections must clearly frame this distinction. The single most important public-health message in the underlying brief.

## The two-week rule

For postpartum content: tearfulness, mood lability, mild anxiety in the first ~2 weeks postpartum is usually baby blues (50–80% prevalence, self-limiting). Past 2 weeks, or severe at any point, or impairing function — treat as possible PND and screen, not blues. Use this as the dividing line.

## Postpartum psychosis — emergency content for the relevant weeks

Late third trimester and postpartum weeks should include this in the *Red flags* paragraph:

- Severe insomnia even when given the chance to sleep, in the first 2–4 weeks postpartum
- Confusion, rapid mood shifts, grandiosity, or paranoia
- Bizarre delusions, especially involving the baby (and *believed*, not distressing)
- Hallucinations
- This is a 999 / 911 / emergency-department situation. Largest single risk factor: bipolar disorder. Prior postpartum psychosis = roughly >50% recurrence without prophylaxis (Bergink cohort, ~10% with prophylactic lithium).

Always frame as "this is rare (1–2 per 1,000) and recognisable" — never bury it, never alarm with it.

## Crisis resources

Surface where appropriate (don't bloat — name once when relevant, in *Look after yourself too* or red flags):

- **UK:** NHS 111 (option 2 for mental health), Samaritans 116 123, PANDAS Foundation 0808 1961 776, Action on Postpartum Psychosis (app-network.org)
- **US:** 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline), Postpartum Support International 1-800-944-4773 / postpartum.net (with PSI Help for Dads at postpartum.net/get-help/help-for-dads), National Maternal Mental Health Hotline 1-833-852-6262
- **Always:** emergency department / 999 / 911 for imminent danger

## "No treatment" is not the safe default

Untreated antenatal depression has documented risks of its own: preterm birth, low birth weight, child cognitive and behavioural effects, elevated maternal suicide risk. The medication decision is not "medication risk vs no risk" — it's "medication risk vs untreated-illness risk", with both arms real.

The weekly file does **not** advise on medication directly. It can name that this is a both-sides decision and direct to specialist perinatal mental health care. Sertraline is generally first-line in pregnancy and lactation when medication is indicated — the brief covers this; the weekly file does not need to.

## Red lines — never include

- Phrases that minimise: "at least it's not as bad as..." or "it'll pass"
- Phrases that imply gratitude is required: "remember how lucky we are"
- Anything that suggests the partner should "manage" or "handle" her emotions
- Suggestions to override her stated wishes about the pregnancy
- Detailed psychiatric diagnostic criteria — keep warning signs descriptive ("can't enjoy anything for weeks", not "anhedonia"), and always route to her GP/care provider

## Tone calibration

Aim for: a thoughtful friend who's been through it talking to a partner who genuinely wants to do well.

Not: clinical, preachy, performatively woke, sentimental, or written like a self-help book.
