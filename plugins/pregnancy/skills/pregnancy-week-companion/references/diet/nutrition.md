# Nutrition

The food, vitamins, minerals, and supplement layer of the weekly file. This reference is the spine for any nutrition content that ends up in a weekly output — `Mum's body` (when a symptom has a nutritional lever), `For your partner` (food prep, what to bring), `This week's to-dos` (supplements, screening, lifestyle), and the `Baby this week` detailed breakdown (when a nutrient ties directly to what's developing).

The user wants science-based, mechanism-first content. That means: explain *why* a nutrient matters (what it builds, what it protects against), not "eat healthy". Present supplements and food choices as options anchored in evidence, not as a prescriptive checklist. Avoid moralising about food.

## How to use this file

1. **Don't put a "nutrition section" in every week.** There is no nutrition section in the template. Nutrition surfaces *through* the existing six sections when something is specifically relevant that week — a developmental milestone that depends on a nutrient (folate at 4–6w, choline 8–20w, DHA 24w+, iron throughout), a symptom with a food lever (nausea, heartburn, constipation, leg cramps, fatigue), a screening test (GTT at 24–28w), or a partner action (morning tray, freezer-stocking).
2. **Triangulate with Tier 1 sources.** NHS, NICE NG201, Mayo, ACOG, and RCOG are the spine for "what's evidence-based" on supplements and food safety. Use this file's structure but cross-check specific dose claims against those bodies for the country/context.
3. **Mechanism first.** When mentioning a nutrient, say briefly what it's for. "Iron — blood volume expands 40–50% during pregnancy, so requirements roughly double" lands harder than "iron is important".
4. **Options, not prescriptions.** Supplement brand recommendations from `vittorio-protocol.md` are *one* sensible option. The page should usually say "a prenatal with X, Y, Z" not "buy FullWell". Brand specifics only when the user asks or it's clearly in scope.
5. **No moralising.** Mention food restrictions once, factually, where they're relevant. Don't repeat "no alcohol" every week.
6. **Postpartum nutrition matters too.** This skill covers up to week 40, but several weekly to-dos in the third trimester point toward postpartum (freezer-stocking, breastfeeding supply, recovery). The postpartum section below feeds those.

## The core nutrients (what each one does, where to get it)

This is the working set. Roughly in order of how often they'll show up in weekly files.

### Folate / folic acid
- **What it does:** Builds the neural tube — the structure that becomes the brain and spinal cord. The neural tube closes 21–28 days post-conception (weeks 5–6 LMP), often before pregnancy is confirmed. Folate deficiency in this window causes neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly). Folate also supports rapid cell division throughout pregnancy and DNA methylation — relevant to fetal programming.
- **Daily target:** 400–800 µg/day. NHS/NICE recommend 400 µg from before conception through week 12, then continue if possible. Higher doses (5 mg/day) prescribed for women with diabetes, on antiepileptics, BMI ≥30, or previous NTD-affected pregnancy.
- **Form matters:** Methylfolate (5-MTHF) or folinic acid are preferred if MTHFR variant status is unknown or positive. Folic acid is the synthetic form that the liver converts; works for most people, less efficient for the ~30–50% with MTHFR variants. Doesn't hurt to default to methylfolate.
- **Food sources:** Leafy greens (spinach, kale), lentils, beans, chickpeas, asparagus, broccoli, citrus, fortified breads/cereals. Food folate is harder to hit the target with alone — supplementation is standard of care.
- **Week-relevance:** Critical pre-conception → ~12 weeks. Mention specifically at weeks 4–6 (the closure window) and weeks 8–10 (the still-forming-spinal-cord weeks).

### Choline
- **What it does:** A precursor to acetylcholine (neurotransmitter), phosphatidylcholine (cell membranes), and a methyl donor (DNA expression). Critical for fetal brain development, especially hippocampal development (memory). Maternal supply is the only supply — the fetus can't synthesise enough.
- **Daily target:** 450 mg/day during pregnancy, 550 mg/day during lactation. Most women hit ~half of this from diet alone.
- **The problem:** Most prenatals contain little to no choline (5% or less of the target). This is one of the most under-dosed nutrients in standard prenatal care.
- **Food sources:** Eggs are by far the best — one large egg yolk delivers ~150 mg, so three eggs/day covers most of the target. Beef liver (huge — ~350 mg per 100 g), salmon, chicken, soybeans, cauliflower, broccoli. Plant sources are much lower.
- **Supplement:** If eggs aren't tolerated (common in first-trimester nausea), citicoline (CDP-choline) or alpha-GPC supplements close the gap. Vittorio names Jarrow Citicoline as one option.
- **Week-relevance:** Brain-relevant from the start, but the user-facing message lands best around weeks 8–20 when neurogenesis is intense and the hippocampus is forming. Useful to mention in the brain development bullet whenever a memory- or cognition-related milestone is occurring.

### Iron
- **What it does:** Builds maternal red blood cells (blood volume expands 40–50%), supports placental development, transports oxygen to the fetus, and the baby builds her own iron stores in the third trimester to last her first 6 months. Iron deficiency in pregnancy is linked to preterm birth, low birth weight, postpartum depression, and infant iron-deficiency anaemia.
- **Daily target:** ~27 mg/day during pregnancy (vs ~18 mg non-pregnant). About 1,000 mg total extra iron is needed across the pregnancy.
- **Check ferritin, not just haemoglobin.** Haemoglobin drops naturally with the plasma volume expansion (haemodilution) — that's not anaemia. Ferritin is the storage marker. Target ferritin >30 ng/mL pre-conception, ideally 50+. NHS routinely checks Hb at booking and ~28 weeks; ferritin often needs to be requested.
- **Forms:** Iron bisglycinate (gentler on the gut), ferrous sulfate (cheaper, more constipation), heme iron from food (best absorbed). Take with vitamin C, away from calcium, tea, coffee — these inhibit absorption.
- **Food sources:** Red meat (especially liver), shellfish, sardines, lentils, beans, fortified cereals, dark leafy greens. Heme iron (animal sources) absorbs at ~25%, non-heme (plant) at ~5–15%.
- **Week-relevance:** Booking bloods (~8–12w), 28-week repeat screen, anytime fatigue is severe enough to mention. Third-trimester emphasis because the baby is loading up her own stores.

### Iodine
- **What it does:** Required for thyroid hormones (T3, T4), which are essential for fetal brain development — particularly cortical neuron migration in the second trimester. Deficiency causes preventable cognitive impairment; severe deficiency causes cretinism. The UK is mildly iodine-deficient as a population.
- **Daily target:** 150 µg/day pre-conception and during pregnancy, 200 µg/day during lactation.
- **Food sources:** Sea fish, shellfish, dairy, eggs, seaweed (but watch — kelp can be wildly over-dosed; avoid kelp supplements).
- **Supplement:** Most prenatals include 150 µg as potassium iodide. Vegans/those who don't eat dairy or fish need to actively supplement.
- **Week-relevance:** Mention at booking (when prenatal is being checked), and once around 14–16w (when fetal thyroid starts producing its own hormone but still depends heavily on maternal supply).

### Vitamin D
- **What it does:** Calcium absorption, bone development, immune regulation. Deficiency is linked to preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, preterm birth, low birth weight. Low maternal vitamin D leads to low infant vitamin D — which is why exclusively breastfed infants are usually given drops.
- **Daily target:** 600 IU/day per ACOG; NHS recommends 10 µg (400 IU). Many practitioners now suggest higher (1,000–4,000 IU) if deficient. Target blood level 30–50 ng/mL minimum, some target 50–80.
- **Postpartum/breastfeeding:** Standard 400 IU doesn't enrich breast milk meaningfully. Higher maternal doses (e.g. 6,400 IU as in the Wagner 2015 trial) raise breast milk vitamin D enough to remove the need for infant drops. Discuss the dose with a provider — this exceeds some labels but the trial evidence is solid.
- **Food sources:** Oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), egg yolks, fortified foods. Sun on bare skin is the historical source but season, latitude, sunscreen, and time indoors mean most adults don't make enough.
- **Week-relevance:** Booking bloods (request 25(OH)D), repeat in third trimester if low. Mention when discussing bone development (around 12–16w when ossification ramps), and when planning for postpartum.

### Omega-3 / DHA
- **What it does:** DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) makes up ~30% of the structural fat in the fetal brain and retina. Maternal stores are what gets transferred to the baby. Adequate DHA is linked to better visual and cognitive outcomes; deficiency to higher preterm risk.
- **Daily target:** 200–300 mg DHA/day minimum, 600 mg often suggested. Combined EPA+DHA from 2–3 portions of oily fish per week meets most of this if fish is being eaten.
- **Food sources:** Salmon (wild lower in mercury than farmed for some species), sardines, anchovies, mackerel (not king mackerel — too high in mercury), trout, herring. Plant sources (flax, chia, walnuts) provide ALA which converts to DHA inefficiently (~5%).
- **Mercury caution:** Limit high-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, marlin, king mackerel, bigeye tuna, tilefish — these accumulate methylmercury, which is neurotoxic to the developing brain). Low-mercury options (salmon, sardines, anchovies, herring) are encouraged.
- **Supplement:** Algae-based DHA (vegan) or fish oil, third-party tested for heavy metals. 200–600 mg DHA/day if fish intake is low.
- **Week-relevance:** Mention in the brain development bullet from ~18 weeks onward, particularly third trimester when fetal brain growth is fastest and DHA deposition peaks.

### Calcium
- **What it does:** Builds fetal skeleton (especially third trimester when ossification accelerates), supports maternal cardiovascular adaptation, may reduce preeclampsia risk. The fetus will leach calcium from maternal bones if intake is inadequate.
- **Daily target:** 1,000 mg/day pregnancy (1,300 mg if under 19). Increases postpartum, especially with breastfeeding.
- **Food sources:** Dairy (yoghurt, cheese, milk — full-fat is fine), tinned sardines with bones, dark leafy greens (kale, bok choy), fortified plant milks, almonds, tofu (calcium-set), tahini.
- **Absorption notes:** Don't take with iron supplements (compete for absorption). Vitamin D and magnesium support absorption.
- **Week-relevance:** Less prominent in early weeks. Worth mentioning around 20–24w when skeletal mineralisation accelerates, and in the third trimester.

### Magnesium
- **What it does:** Smooth muscle relaxation, blood pressure regulation, sleep quality, glucose metabolism. Deficiency may contribute to leg cramps, restless legs, constipation, and some forms of insomnia.
- **Daily target:** 350–400 mg/day. Most diets undershoot.
- **Food sources:** Pumpkin seeds, almonds, cashews, dark chocolate, spinach, black beans, edamame, avocado.
- **Supplement:** Magnesium glycinate (gentle, sleep-supportive) or citrate (constipation-helpful). Avoid magnesium oxide (poorly absorbed, mostly laxative).
- **Week-relevance:** When leg cramps appear (typically late second / early third trimester), constipation, sleep issues, restless legs.

### B12
- **What it does:** Cofactor with folate in DNA synthesis and methylation; needed for neurological development and red blood cell formation. Vegans and long-term vegetarians often run low; severe maternal deficiency causes infant neurological damage.
- **Daily target:** 2.6 µg/day pregnancy, 2.8 µg/day lactation.
- **Food sources:** Animal foods only — meat, fish, eggs, dairy. Plant sources (some seaweed, fortified foods) are unreliable.
- **Supplement:** Methylcobalamin or hydroxocobalamin preferred over cyanocobalamin. Test at booking, especially for plant-based diets.

### Zinc
- **What it does:** Cell division, immune function, DNA synthesis. Deficiency linked to preterm birth, low birth weight, longer labour.
- **Daily target:** 11 mg/day pregnancy.
- **Food sources:** Oysters (very high), beef, lamb, pumpkin seeds, lentils, chickpeas, cashews.

### Protein
- **What it does:** The structural raw material for everything the baby builds — placenta, uterus, baby's own tissue, expanded maternal blood volume.
- **Daily target:** ~1.2 g/kg/day during pregnancy (vs ~0.8 g/kg non-pregnant). For an average 70 kg woman that's ~80–100 g/day. Most women undershoot this — particularly in the first trimester when meat aversion is common, and women relying on toast/crackers for nausea management.
- **Food sources:** Eggs, meat, fish, dairy, legumes, tofu, Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese.
- **Week-relevance:** Worth mentioning in the second trimester (the build phase) and connecting back to the first-trimester nausea protocol (protein and fat — not just crackers — keep blood sugar steady).

### Fibre + fluids
- **What they do:** Manage pregnancy constipation (progesterone slows gut motility from very early on). Hydration also helps with headaches, blood pressure regulation, and amniotic fluid volume.
- **Daily target:** 25–30 g fibre, 2.3 L fluid (more if hot climate or active).
- **Food sources:** Whole grains, oats, beans, lentils, fruit (especially with skin), vegetables, chia, flax.
- **Week-relevance:** Constipation is a very common, rarely-volunteered complaint. Mention practically — water + fibre + walking — when it fits.

## The non-negotiable supplement spine

This is the evidence-supported baseline. If a prenatal contains these in adequate doses, supplementation beyond it is optional and condition-specific.

- **Folate/folic acid:** 400 µg/day (5 mg if high-risk groups, on medical advice). Start pre-conception, continue through at least week 12, ideally through pregnancy.
- **Iodine:** 150 µg/day (200 µg lactation). Usually in the prenatal.
- **Vitamin D:** 10 µg (400 IU)/day minimum per NHS; many practitioners suggest 1,000–4,000 IU. Test status if possible.
- **Iron:** Only routinely if deficient. Don't supplement blindly — excess iron has its own harms.
- **DHA:** If oily fish isn't being eaten 2–3x/week, 200–600 mg/day supplement.
- **Choline:** Only adequately covered by diet (eggs); citicoline supplement if eggs aren't tolerated.
- **B12:** Required for vegan/vegetarian diets, often included in prenatal.

A reasonable prenatal hits folate, iodine, B12, zinc, vitamin D. Choline, DHA, and iron usually need to be assessed separately.

## What to avoid (and why, briefly)

The list below should be mentioned **once**, factually, where it fits — usually in the booking-appointment to-do around weeks 6–8, and revisited only if a specific food/substance is week-relevant. Don't moralise. Don't repeat every week.

- **Alcohol:** No safe lower threshold has been established. Crosses the placenta freely. Risk is dose-dependent across all of pregnancy; first trimester is the most sensitive window for structural development, but neurodevelopment continues to be affected throughout. NHS/RCOG recommend zero. Worth a brief, non-judgemental mention pre-conception or at the booking-appointment to-do.
- **Smoking, vaping, recreational drugs (including secondhand smoke):** Vasoconstriction, growth restriction, preterm risk, SIDS risk, cognitive effects. Same principle: no safe threshold.
- **High-mercury fish:** Shark, swordfish, marlin, king mackerel, bigeye tuna, tilefish. Methylmercury is neurotoxic to the developing brain. Replace with salmon, sardines, anchovies, herring.
- **Raw/undercooked meat, raw fish/sushi, raw eggs:** Listeria, salmonella, toxoplasmosis. Risk is small but consequences can be severe (miscarriage, stillbirth, neurological damage in survivors).
- **Unpasteurised soft cheeses (brie, camembert, blue-veined, soft goat's cheese with rind):** Listeria. Hard cheeses, pasteurised soft cheeses are fine.
- **Pâté (all types, including vegetable):** Listeria + sometimes high vitamin A in liver pâté.
- **Liver and high-dose vitamin A:** Retinol in large doses is teratogenic. Eat liver occasionally if at all (once every couple of weeks max), avoid retinol supplements above prenatal doses.
- **Room-temperature deli meats, cold smoked fish (if not heated):** Listeria. Heat to steaming if eating.
- **Caffeine:** Under 200 mg/day (about one strong coffee or two cups of tea). Crosses the placenta; the fetus can't metabolise it. Higher doses are linked to miscarriage and low birth weight.
- **Unwashed fruit/veg, unpasteurised juice and milk:** Toxoplasmosis, listeria, E. coli risk.
- **Herbal teas in large amounts:** Many haven't been studied. Avoid liquorice root (high amounts linked to preterm birth), avoid raspberry leaf before late third trimester (uterine stimulant — evidence weak either way). Ginger, peppermint, rooibos are generally considered safe.
- **High doses of any single vitamin or mineral** (especially A, D, iron, iodine) without testing — supplementation isn't risk-free.
- **Some skincare:** High-dose retinoids (tretinoin, retinol, retinyl palmitate), salicylic acid in oral or high-dose topical form, hydroquinone. Most other ingredients are fine.
- **Plastic + heat:** BPA and phthalates are endocrine disruptors. Don't heat food in plastic. Glass storage where possible. Receipts are coated in BPA — wash hands after handling.

## Things commonly worried about that are usually fine

- **Coffee in moderation (one strong cup/day or equivalent).**
- **Cooked sushi rolls (tempura, fully-cooked seafood).** Raw is the issue.
- **Hard cheeses, pasteurised soft cheeses, halloumi, feta from pasteurised milk.**
- **Cured meats** (parma ham, prosciutto, salami) — listeria risk is low but real. Some authorities advise heating; some include them; check the local guidance you're working from. Mention the trade-off, don't catastrophise.
- **Fermented foods (live yoghurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut) made commercially with pasteurised inputs.**
- **Honey** — fine for the mother. Not before 12 months for the baby (infant botulism).
- **Spicy food, garlic, ginger:** All fine. Won't induce labour (despite folklore).
- **Artificial sweeteners in moderation:** Aspartame, sucralose, stevia considered safe. Saccharin crosses placenta, generally avoided.

## The first-trimester nausea protocol (food-focused)

The mechanism, in one paragraph: First-trimester nausea is hormonally driven (hCG peaking weeks 8–12). It can't be eaten away. But it can be made dramatically worse by blood sugar volatility. Pregnancy raises insulin resistance, so glucose spikes (from crackers, juice, sugary cereal) cause bigger insulin overshoots and bigger crashes. The crash triggers cortisol, which triggers another nausea wave. Standard "keep crackers by the bed" advice runs this loop on purpose. Protein and fat don't spike glucose — they release it slowly. Steady glucose, fewer triggers stacking on the hormonal baseline.

**The working rule:** protein and fat before sleep, protein and fat first thing on waking, never let the body run empty overnight.

**The nightly bowl (before sleep):** Full-fat Greek yoghurt + berries + a drizzle of honey. Slow protein + fat overnight, with a small glucose bridge.

**The morning tray (before she's fully awake, plated by the partner):**
- Sourdough toast + avocado + two soft-scrambled eggs (the eggs alone cover ~300 mg of the 450 mg choline target)
- Cottage cheese + prosciutto + cherry tomatoes
- Parmesan shavings + bresaola + figs
- Soft-boiled egg + sardines on toast
- Full-fat ricotta + walnuts + honey + a pear
- Smoked salmon + cream cheese + cucumber on rye

**Escalation ladder if nausea persists despite the food protocol:**
1. Ginger 1 g/day in divided doses (capsules or fresh ginger tea — both work)
2. Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) 25 mg three times daily
3. Unisom (doxylamine) 12.5 mg at night + B6 — the OTC equivalent of Diclegis, category A in pregnancy
4. Electrolyte hydration (LMNT-style salt + sugar drinks, coconut water, salt + lime water — plain water often makes nausea worse)
5. Sea-Bands at P6 acupressure point (placebo or real — if it works, use it)

**Escalation to medical care:** If she can't keep any food or fluid down for 24 hours, is losing weight rapidly, or shows ketones in urine — this is hyperemesis gravidarum, which is a different condition. IV fluids and ondansetron (Zofran) exist and are used. Don't white-knuckle hyperemesis.

## Trimester-by-trimester focus

### First trimester (weeks 4–13)
- **Nutrient priorities:** Folate (neural tube), choline (brain), iodine (thyroid), B12.
- **Practical reality:** Many women can barely eat. Don't optimise — survive. Anything she can keep down is good. Re-introduce protein and fat as nausea allows. The partner's job is to find the foods that work *today* (it changes daily) and have them ready.
- **What to avoid in this trimester especially:** Alcohol (most sensitive window for structural development), high-mercury fish, retinol products, raw meat.
- **Booking bloods to request:** Full blood count, ferritin, vitamin D (25-OH), TSH, B12, folate, glucose. Most aren't routine on the NHS — ask.

### Second trimester (weeks 14–27)
- **Nutrient priorities:** Iron (blood volume expanding fast now), choline (neurogenesis at peak ~17–18w LMP), DHA (brain growth ramping), calcium (skeletal mineralisation).
- **Practical reality:** Appetite usually returns. This is the build phase — protein intake matters most here. Aim for ~80–100 g protein/day. Eggs, meat, fish, dairy, legumes.
- **Gestational diabetes screening (24–28w):** Glucose tolerance test. If positive, the eating pattern changes — lower glycaemic load, more fibre, protein at every meal, post-meal walks. Manageable. Will be flagged through standard care.
- **Common complaints with food levers:** Constipation (fibre + fluids + walking + magnesium if needed), heartburn (smaller meals, no eating close to lying down, head of bed elevated), leg cramps (magnesium, calcium, hydration).

### Third trimester (weeks 28–40)
- **Nutrient priorities:** Iron (the baby is loading stores for her first 6 months), DHA (brain growth fastest now), choline, calcium, vitamin D (positioning for postpartum and breast milk enrichment).
- **Practical reality:** Stomach capacity shrinks as the uterus crowds it. Smaller, more frequent meals work better. Heartburn often peaks. Energy demands rise — eating to fullness, not restriction.
- **Specific late-pregnancy items (if the user wants them):**
  - **Dates from ~36 weeks:** Six dates per day (~70–75 g). One trial found arrival at hospital more dilated (3.5 cm vs 2 cm), 96% spontaneous labour onset vs 79%, shorter latent phase. Dates contain oxytocin-like compounds, potassium, magnesium, tannins. Cheap, safe, and the evidence is modest but real. Mention as an option, not a prescription.
  - **Stocking the freezer:** Practical priority from ~34 weeks. Cooked meals for the first 2–3 weeks postpartum.
- **What to avoid in this trimester:** Large gaps between meals (worsens heartburn and reflux), restrictive eating "because she's gained weight" (the gain is the design — placenta, blood volume, baby, fluid).

## Postpartum nutrition (4th trimester preview)

The skill covers up to week 40, but several weekly to-dos in the third trimester gesture forward to the fourth. This section is here so those gestures are accurate.

### What the body is doing
- Recovering from the largest physiological event of her life
- Producing milk (~500–700 extra kcal/day burned by breastfeeding once established)
- Healing tissue (perineum, possibly C-section incision, uterus involuting)
- Re-establishing maternal iron stores (blood loss at birth + ongoing lochia)
- Recalibrating hormones (oestrogen and progesterone crash within 48h; prolactin and oxytocin take over)

### Nutrient priorities postpartum / breastfeeding
- **Iron:** Often depleted further by birth blood loss. Retest ferritin at the 6-week check.
- **Vitamin D:** As above — higher maternal dose can enrich breast milk enough to remove the need for infant drops. Wagner 2015 trial used 6,400 IU/day. Discuss the specific dose with a provider.
- **Choline:** Lactation target rises to 550 mg/day. Eggs continue to do the heavy lifting.
- **DHA:** Continues to transfer through breast milk. 200–600 mg/day. Linked to infant cognitive outcomes.
- **Iodine:** Lactation target 200 µg/day.
- **Calcium:** 1,000 mg/day. Breast milk will be calcium-adequate even at the cost of maternal bone if intake is low — protect mum's bones by hitting the target.
- **Protein:** 1.3 g/kg/day or more. Tissue repair + milk production.
- **Hydration:** Breastfeeding raises fluid needs significantly. Thirst is a reliable cue if she's listening to it; otherwise aim for ~3 L/day.
- **Creatine (5–10 g/day):** Vittorio includes this; the evidence base for transfer rate into breast milk hasn't been formally measured, so this is a considered risk-benefit call by the user. Mention only if the user wants it; not a default recommendation.

### What "bad" postpartum diet looks like, and what it costs
Worth being honest about, because this is genuinely under-discussed.

- **Chronic under-eating** (often from being too busy, too tired, or trying to "lose the baby weight" early) → milk supply drops, mood drops, hair shedding accelerates, recovery slows. The "eating for two" line is wrong during pregnancy but closer to true during breastfeeding.
- **Reliance on ultraprocessed/refined-carb foods** (which is what tends to happen when no one is cooking) → blood sugar swings drive mood and fatigue worse, postpartum thyroid issues are easier to miss against a backdrop of generally feeling rotten, gut microbiome takes longer to recover (relevant if breastfeeding — some of mum's microbiome transfers).
- **Inadequate protein:** Most directly visible cost is slower tissue repair (perineum, incision) and slower return of strength. Less visible: ongoing fatigue, hair shedding, mood.
- **Skipping the prenatal/postnatal supplement:** Folate matters less now, but iron, B12, vitamin D, choline, DHA still matter — especially if breastfeeding.
- **High alcohol intake:** Crosses into breast milk; also worsens sleep architecture, which is already a scarce resource. Occasional small drink with timing (an hour before a feed, then expressing/discarding if the next feed is soon) is manageable; daily/regular drinking compounds sleep deprivation and increases PPD risk.
- **Aggressive restrictive dieting in the first 3–6 months:** Drops milk supply, worsens mood, risks rebound. Weight returns gradually — most healthy postpartum recovery is months, not weeks.

### Postpartum eating that supports recovery
- Protein at every meal (eggs, meat, fish, yoghurt, cottage cheese, lentils)
- Iron-rich foods + vitamin C source
- A daily portion of oily fish or DHA supplement
- Eggs every day if tolerated
- Magnesium-rich foods or supplement for sleep quality and constipation
- Bone broth, slow-cooked meats (warming, easy to eat one-handed, traditionally favoured in many cultures' postpartum diets for reasons that line up with the nutrient density — collagen, glycine, minerals)
- Snacks accessible at every nursing station: nuts, cheese, jerky, fruit, hard-boiled eggs

### Practical (this is where partner action shows up in late-third-trimester to-dos)
- Stock the freezer from ~34 weeks. Cooked, portioned, labelled. 2–3 weeks of meals if you can manage it.
- Build 2–3 "nursing stations" around the house with water, snacks, charger, burp cloths.
- "Say yes to food drops" — friends and family will offer. Take them.

## How nutrition surfaces in each section of a weekly file

A quick map so you don't have to invent this every week.

- **`Baby this week` detailed breakdown** — When a specific nutrient ties to what's developing (folate at 4–6w neural tube, choline through neurogenesis weeks 8–20+, iodine at 14–16w fetal thyroid, DHA through brain growth phases, iron in the third trimester for fetal stores). One specific, short mechanism — not a nutrition lesson.
- **`Mum's body` detailed breakdown** — When a symptom has a food/nutrient lever. Nausea (protein/fat protocol), constipation (fibre/fluids/magnesium), leg cramps (magnesium/calcium), heartburn (small meals, no late eating), fatigue (iron — request ferritin), restless legs (iron + magnesium).
- **`For your partner`** — Practical food prep. Morning tray (first trimester), snack stocking, freezer-stocking (late third), what to bring to hospital (jerky, cheese, nuts, electrolyte drinks). The principle: she doesn't make her own food in the first six months postpartum.
- **`This week's to-dos`** — Supplement check at booking, ferritin retest at ~28w, GTT at 24–28w, dietary trial of dates from ~36w. Don't repeat "take your folate" every week.
- **`Red flags`** — Inability to keep food/fluid down for 24h (hyperemesis), severe persistent fatigue (anaemia), severe persistent headache + visual changes (preeclampsia — partial blood pressure / protein in urine connection, food not the lever).
