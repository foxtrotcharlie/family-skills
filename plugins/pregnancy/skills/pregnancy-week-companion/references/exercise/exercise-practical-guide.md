# Exercise During Pregnancy: A Practical Guide

## The Big Picture

Exercise during pregnancy is not just safe — it's actively good for you and your baby. Every major health organization in the world agrees on this. Unless your doctor has told you otherwise because of a specific complication, you should be moving your body regularly throughout your pregnancy.

**The simple goal:** Aim for about 150 minutes of moderate activity per week (that's roughly 30 minutes, 5 days a week — or 20 minutes every day). "Moderate" means you can still hold a conversation while doing it. If you're breathing so hard you can't talk, ease off a bit.

---

## What Exercise Actually Does For You

Here's what the research consistently shows:

- **Cuts your risk of gestational diabetes by about a third.** This is the strongest benefit — well-proven across dozens of studies.
- **Reduces your chance of needing a cesarean section** (about 30% lower odds).
- **Lowers your risk of developing high blood pressure/preeclampsia** during pregnancy.
- **Significantly reduces depression and anxiety** — both during pregnancy and after birth. Walking is particularly effective.
- **Helps manage weight gain** (modest effect — about 1–2 kg less overall, but mainly prevents *excessive* gain).
- **Reduces back pain and pelvic pain.**
- **Helps you recover faster after birth.**

And crucially: **exercise does NOT increase your risk of miscarriage, preterm birth, or harm to your baby.** This is one of the most studied questions in obstetrics, and the answer is consistently reassuring.

---

## What's Safe To Do

✅ **Great options throughout pregnancy:**
- Walking (the simplest and one of the most effective)
- Swimming and water aerobics (the water supports your weight — takes about 83% of the load off your joints)
- Stationary cycling
- Low-impact aerobics or dance
- Resistance training (weights, resistance bands)
- Yoga and Pilates (regular versions, NOT hot)
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) — do these daily

---

## What To Avoid

🚫 **Don't do these:**
- Contact sports (anything where you could get hit in the stomach)
- Scuba diving (the pressure creates bubbles that your baby can't clear)
- Sports with high fall risk (horse riding, downhill skiing, gymnastics)
- Hot yoga or hot Pilates (overheating is risky, especially early on)
- Lying flat on your back for exercise after about 16–20 weeks (the weight of your uterus compresses a major blood vessel)

## Trimester-by-Trimester Guide

### First Trimester (Weeks 1–13)

**What's happening to your body:** Fatigue, nausea, and hormonal upheaval. You may not feel like doing much — and that's okay.

**What to do:**
- If you were active before pregnancy, **keep doing what you were doing.** No need to dial back unless it feels wrong.
- If you weren't active before, **start gentle.** A 10–15 minute walk is a great beginning. Build up gradually over weeks.
- Focus on establishing a habit rather than hitting a target.
- Start pelvic floor exercises now — they'll pay off later.

**Watch out for:**
- Avoid overheating. Skip the sauna, hot tub, and hot yoga. Your baby's neural tube is forming and excess heat is a concern in these early weeks.
- Stay hydrated.
- If nausea makes exercise impossible on some days, don't force it. Come back to it when you can.

**Week-by-week suggestion:**
- Weeks 5–8: If nausea is bad, even a 10-minute walk counts. Don't feel guilty about doing less.
- Weeks 9–13: Energy often starts returning. Try to build toward 20–30 minutes of activity, 3–5 days per week.

---

### Second Trimester (Weeks 14–27)

**What's happening to your body:** This is often called the "golden trimester." Nausea typically fades, energy returns, and your bump isn't yet big enough to limit movement.

**What to do:**
- This is your best window to build fitness and consistency.
- Aim for the full 150 minutes/week: 30 minutes, 5 days a week is ideal.
- Mix it up: some days walking or swimming (aerobic), some days light weights or resistance bands (strength), daily pelvic floor work.
- Swimming becomes especially nice now — the buoyancy relieves the growing pressure on joints and back.

**Watch out for:**
- **After about week 16–20:** Stop doing exercises lying flat on your back. Modify crunches, some yoga poses, and bench exercises. Prop yourself at an angle or switch to side-lying.
- Your center of gravity is shifting. Be mindful of balance — you may wobble more than usual.
- Drink plenty of water before, during, and after exercise.

**Week-by-week suggestion:**
- Weeks 14–16: Ride the energy wave. If you feel good, this is a great time to add a new activity (a prenatal yoga class, swimming, etc.).
- Weeks 17–20: Transition away from any supine (on-your-back) exercises.
- Weeks 21–27: Maintain your routine. If something starts feeling uncomfortable, modify rather than push through.

---

### Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40)

**What's happening to your body:** Your bump is large, your center of gravity is shifted forward, breathlessness increases, and joints loosen further. You'll likely feel heavier and more tired.

**What to do:**
- **Keep moving, but adapt.** Reducing intensity or duration is completely fine.
- Walking and swimming are your best friends now — low impact, easy to scale up or down.
- Continue resistance exercises (lighter weights, more reps if needed).
- Continue pelvic floor exercises.
- Keep the "talk test" as your guide: if you can chat, you're at the right intensity.

**Watch out for:**
- If something hurts, stop. Round ligament pain, pelvic girdle pain, and Braxton Hicks contractions may all show up — learn to distinguish normal discomfort from warning signs.
- Sessions longer than 45 minutes can drop your blood sugar. Eat a snack beforehand or keep sessions shorter.
- Balance is compromised. Avoid activities where a fall is possible.

**Week-by-week suggestion:**
- Weeks 28–32: You may need to slow down from your second-trimester pace. That's normal and expected.
- Weeks 33–36: Focus on maintaining movement rather than performance. Swimming and walking are excellent.
- Weeks 37–40: Keep walking daily if you can. Even 15–20 minutes helps. Some women find "labor prep" exercises (squats, birth ball bouncing, pelvic tilts) helpful, though evidence for inducing labor is weak.

## If You Were Sedentary Before Pregnancy

Start small. The Canadian guidelines say pregnancy is actually an ideal time to begin exercising because you're motivated and seeing a doctor regularly. Here's a simple ramp-up:

| Weeks | Goal |
|-------|------|
| Weeks 1–4 of starting | 10–15 min walking, 3 days/week |
| Weeks 5–8 of starting | 15–20 min walking, 4 days/week |
| Weeks 9–12 of starting | 20–30 min activity, 5 days/week |
| Ongoing | Maintain 150 min/week at a comfortable level |

Even getting halfway to the goal provides real benefits. Something is always better than nothing.

---

## If You Were Already Active Before Pregnancy

You can generally keep doing what you were doing, with these adjustments:
- Drop contact sports and anything with significant fall risk.
- Pay attention to overheating and hydration.
- After ~16–20 weeks, modify supine exercises.
- Listen to your body — some days will be harder. That's okay.
- There's no proven "too much" threshold for healthy pregnancies, but if something feels wrong, ease off and check with your provider.

---

## Stop Exercising and Call Your Doctor If:

- Vaginal bleeding
- Fluid leaking from the vagina
- Regular, painful contractions
- Chest pain
- Dizziness or feeling faint
- Sudden headache
- Calf pain or swelling (could indicate a blood clot)
- Shortness of breath *before* you even start
- Muscle weakness that affects your balance
- Significant abdominal pain

---

## The Bottom Line

1. **Move regularly.** 150 minutes/week of moderate activity is the target, but any movement counts.
2. **Walking is genuinely excellent.** It's free, requires no equipment, and the research shows it's one of the most effective exercises for both physical and mental health during pregnancy.
3. **Swimming is a gift in later pregnancy.** The water holds you up.
4. **Do your pelvic floor exercises.** Every day. They prevent incontinence and support recovery.
5. **Listen to your body.** Modify as needed. Some weeks will be harder than others.
6. **Don't worry about harming your baby.** The evidence is overwhelmingly clear that exercise is safe for uncomplicated pregnancies.
7. **If in doubt, ask your provider** — especially if you have any complications or conditions on the contraindications list.
