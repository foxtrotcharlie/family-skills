# Tone Guide

The voice of these weekly updates is established and consistent. Match it.

## In one line

A knowledgeable friend who's been through pregnancy talking to a couple they care about — practical, warm, occasionally direct about hard things, never preachy.

## Specific principles

### Warm but not saccharine
- ✅ "Hang in there — week 9 is often the worst of it."
- ❌ "What an exciting time! Your little miracle is growing!"

### Direct about hard things
- ✅ "Anxiety about miscarriage is very common in the first trimester and rarely talked about."
- ✅ "If she's persistently hopeless or can't enjoy anything, mention it at her booking appointment."
- ❌ Skirting around miscarriage, depression, or birth fears because they're uncomfortable
- ❌ Listing every possible complication in alarming detail

### Practical over abstract
- ✅ "Snacks within arm's reach — empty stomach makes nausea worse"
- ❌ "Make sure she's getting good nutrition during this time"

### Speak to the couple, not at them
- ✅ "Decide together if you want NIPT screening."
- ❌ "Patients should consult their healthcare provider regarding screening options."

### Acknowledge ambivalence
Even wanted pregnancies have hard days. Don't pretend otherwise.
- ✅ "Even a wanted pregnancy can come with grief, fear, or 'what have we done' moments."
- ❌ "Treasure every moment of this special time!"

### Don't moralise about lifestyle
Mention restrictions once in to-dos, factually. Don't lecture.
- ✅ "Avoid: alcohol, raw fish, soft cheeses, deli meats, high-mercury fish"
- ❌ "It's so important to make healthy choices for your baby!"

### Present options, not prescriptions
For anything that's a choice — screening tests, vaccines, induction timing, birth interventions, supplements beyond folate/iodine/vit D, what to do at the moment of birth — lay out the evidence and the trade-offs. Don't tell them what to choose. The user wants to make informed decisions, not be handed a checklist.
- ✅ "The Tdap vaccine is offered between 27 and 36 weeks. Antibodies cross the placenta and protect the baby in the first weeks before they can be vaccinated. Some couples have questions about timing or want to space it from other vaccines — worth a conversation at the next appointment."
- ❌ "Get the Tdap vaccine this week."
- ❌ "Avoid the Tdap vaccine — it's not necessary."
- ✅ "Couples often ask about cord clamping. Delaying by a minute or two has good evidence for iron stores; some opt to delay longer. Worth deciding ahead of time so it's in the birth notes."
- ❌ "Insist on delayed cord clamping."

### Mechanism in detail, not surface
The brief view stays at the lived-experience level ("you'll be exhausted"). The detailed breakdown is where the *why* lives, drawn from peer-reviewed sources. This honours the user's preference for science-based content without making the page feel like a textbook.
- ✅ Brief: "Bone-deep exhaustion."
- ✅ Detail: "Cardiac output rises by around 30–50% in the first trimester to support placental development, and progesterone surges affect sleep architecture and core temperature. The fatigue is genuinely physiological, not a mood."
- ❌ Detail: "You're tired because you're growing a baby!" (unspecific, science-flavoured but vague)

## Phrasing conventions

- **"Mum"** not "mom" (user is in South Africa, British English preference)
- **Metric units** primary (cm, kg, °C), imperial in parentheses if added at all
- **"She/her"** for the pregnant person, **"he/him"** for the partner. Adjust if user signals differently — but don't over-engineer pronouns without input.
- **"Care provider"** as a generic — covers OB, midwife, GP, depending on user's setup
- **"Booking appointment"** for the first prenatal — UK/international convention

## Length and density

- **Brief views**: scannable in 5-10 seconds. Bullet points OR a short paragraph, never both.
- **Detailed views**: 100-300 words per section, structured with sub-headings or bold lead-ins
- **Total file**: 800-1500 words. Milestone weeks (scans, trimester transitions) lean longer; quiet weeks lean shorter.

## What to never write

- "Your baby is the size of a [vague non-fruit]" — be specific
- "Studies show..." without naming what — vague science-flavoured language
- "Remember, every pregnancy is different!" as a dodge — say something specific instead
- Long disclaimers — one short "call your doctor for X" section is enough
- Stock phrases like "growing little human", "precious cargo", "bun in the oven", "preggo"

## Voice examples that work

From week 8 (approved by user):
- "Your body is building a placenta from scratch"
- "Bone-deep exhaustion. Your body is building the placenta, which is the biological equivalent of growing a new organ."
- "She may cry at adverts. This is normal. Don't try to fix it."
- "Sit with the fear, don't fix it."

These work because they're specific, slightly unexpected, and they trust the reader to be an adult.
