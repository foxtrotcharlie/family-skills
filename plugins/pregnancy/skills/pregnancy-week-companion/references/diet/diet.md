# Nutrition and Diet During Pregnancy: Evidence-Based Guidelines, Key Nutrients, and Food Safety

## Executive Summary

Adequate maternal nutrition is a critical modifiable factor for healthy pregnancy outcomes. Major guidelines (ACOG, WHO, NHS, IOM) converge on several key principles: eat a varied diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and dairy; take a daily prenatal supplement (particularly folic acid, iron, iodine, and vitamin D); avoid specific high-risk foods (raw/undercooked meats, high-mercury fish, certain soft cheeses, liver); limit caffeine to ≤200 mg/day; and completely avoid alcohol [1][2][3][5]. Caloric needs increase modestly — no extra calories in the first trimester, +340 kcal/day in the second, and +452 kcal/day in the third [6][7]. Specific nutrient deficiencies are linked to serious outcomes: folate deficiency to neural tube defects, iron deficiency to maternal anemia and preterm birth, and inadequate DHA to suboptimal fetal brain development [2][8]. The Mediterranean dietary pattern has emerging evidence for reducing gestational diabetes risk [9][10][11].

## 1. Current Dietary Guidelines

### 1.1 ACOG (United States)

ACOG recommends [1]:
- A daily prenatal supplement containing folic acid, iron, and other key nutrients.
- A diet including all five food groups: grains, fruits, vegetables, protein, and dairy.
- Screening patients regarding diet and supplement use to ensure recommended intakes for calcium, iron, vitamin A, B vitamins, vitamin D, and other nutrients.
- Limiting caffeine to <200 mg/day.
- Complete avoidance of alcohol.

### 1.2 WHO

WHO antenatal care recommendations include [3][4]:
- Counselling about healthy eating and physical activity to stay healthy and prevent excessive weight gain.
- A healthy diet with adequate energy, protein, vitamins, and minerals from a variety of foods: green and orange vegetables, meat, fish, beans, nuts, whole grains, and fruit.
- In undernourished populations: nutrition education on increasing daily energy and protein intake to reduce low-birth-weight risk.
- Daily iron and folic acid supplementation (30–60 mg iron + 400 mcg folic acid) to reduce maternal anemia, puerperal sepsis, low birth weight, and preterm birth.
- Multiple micronutrient supplements as an alternative to iron-folic acid where available [16].

### 1.3 NHS (United Kingdom)

NHS guidance focuses on practical food safety [5]:
- Take a supplement with 400 mcg folic acid daily until week 12, and 10 mcg (400 IU) vitamin D throughout pregnancy.
- Eat a balanced diet; no need to "eat for two."
- Specific foods to avoid (detailed in Section 5).
- Caffeine limit: 200 mg/day.
- No alcohol.

### 1.4 US Dietary Guidelines for Americans (2020–2025)

- Recommends a daily prenatal supplement, especially for folate/folic acid, iron, iodine, and vitamin D [2].
- Emphasizes nutrient-dense food choices within calorie limits.
- Identifies calcium and vitamin D as nutrients of public health concern.

## 2. Caloric Needs by Trimester

| Trimester | Extra Calories/Day | Total (typical) | Source |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|--------|
| First (weeks 1–13) | 0 | ~1,800 kcal | [6][7] |
| Second (weeks 14–27) | +340 | ~2,200 kcal | [6][7] |
| Third (weeks 28–40) | +452 | ~2,400 kcal | [6][7] |

- These are for normal-weight women with singleton pregnancies.
- Adjustments needed for underweight, overweight/obese, multiples, or highly active women.
- "Eating for two" does not mean doubling intake — it means approximately one additional healthy snack per day in the second trimester [6][14].

### 3.2 Folate / Folic Acid

- **Why critical:** Prevents neural tube defects (NTDs) — spina bifida, anencephaly. NTD risk reduction with adequate folic acid is 50–70% [2][8].
- **Timing:** Neural tube closes by day 28 — supplementation must begin before conception or very early pregnancy [2].
- **Recommendation:** 400–800 mcg folic acid daily from supplements, starting at least 1 month before conception and continuing through the first trimester. Women with prior NTD-affected pregnancy: 4,000 mcg/day [2][8].
- **UL:** 1,000 mcg from supplements/fortified foods (does not apply to food folate) [2].

### 3.3 Iron

- **Why critical:** Blood volume expands ~50% during pregnancy; iron is needed for hemoglobin production and fetal iron stores [2].
- **Deficiency consequences:** Maternal anemia (27.5% in third trimester in US), preterm birth, low birth weight [8].
- **Recommendation:** 27 mg/day RDA. WHO recommends 30–60 mg/day [3]. Many prenatals contain 27–30 mg.
- **Tips:** Take with vitamin C to enhance absorption; avoid taking with calcium, tea, or coffee which inhibit absorption [2].
- **UL:** 45 mg/day [2].

### 3.4 Calcium

- **Why critical:** Fetal skeleton development; maternal bone health; may reduce preeclampsia risk [2].
- **Evidence:** A 2024 meta-analysis of 26 RCTs (20,038 women): calcium supplementation reduced preeclampsia risk by 49% and gestational hypertension by 30% — primarily in women with low baseline intake [2].
- **Recommendation:** 1,000 mg/day (ages 19–50). In populations with low calcium intake: supplementation of 1,500–2,000 mg/day may be warranted per WHO [2].
- **Note:** Prenatal supplements typically contain only ~200 mg — dietary sources (dairy, fortified foods) also needed [2].

### 3.5 Vitamin D

- **Why critical:** Calcium absorption, bone health, immune function, potentially preeclampsia and preterm birth prevention [2][8].
- **Deficiency prevalence:** Very common — many pregnant women have inadequate vitamin D status, especially in northern latitudes and those with darker skin [8].
- **Official RDA:** 600 IU (15 mcg) [2]. Some researchers recommend 1,000–4,000 IU based on optimization studies [8].
- **Sources:** Fortified milk, fatty fish, egg yolks, sunlight exposure.
- **UL:** 4,000 IU (100 mcg) [2].

### 3.6 Choline

- **Why critical:** Fetal brain development, neural tube closure, placental function [2][8].
- **Major gap:** Most prenatal supplements contain little or no choline. Many women in the US do not meet the AI [2].
- **AI:** 450 mg/day [2].
- **Best sources:** Eggs (1 egg ≈ 150 mg), liver, beef, soybeans, wheat germ.

### 3.7 DHA (Omega-3)

- **Why critical:** Structural component of fetal brain and retina; may reduce preterm birth risk [8].
- **Evidence:** Cochrane review (Middleton 2018) — omega-3 supplementation reduced early preterm birth [8].
- **Recommendation:** 200–300 mg DHA/day (consensus of expert bodies). FDA/EPA advise 2–3 servings of low-mercury fish per week [2][5].
- **Sources:** Salmon, sardines, herring, anchovies; algae-based DHA supplements for vegetarians.
- **Note:** Many prenatal supplements lack DHA — may need separate supplement [2].

### 3.8 Iodine

- **Why critical:** Thyroid hormone production; fetal brain and nervous system development. Severe deficiency causes cretinism [2].
- **Deficiency concern:** Mild-to-moderate iodine deficiency is common, even in developed countries [2].
- **RDA:** 220 mcg/day [2].
- **Sources:** Iodized salt, dairy, fish, seaweed. Some prenatal supplements lack iodine — check the label [2].

## 4. Prenatal Supplements

### What to Look For

Based on ACOG [1], NIH [2], and evidence-based recommendations [8]:

| Nutrient | Minimum in Prenatal | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------|
| Folic acid | 400–800 mcg | As folic acid specifically (for NTD prevention) |
| Iron | 27–30 mg | May cause constipation; take with vitamin C |
| Calcium | 200–300 mg | Usually not enough — supplement with diet |
| Vitamin D | 600–1,000 IU | Consider higher if deficient |
| Iodine | 150–220 mcg | Check label — not in all prenatals |
| DHA | 200–300 mg | Often requires separate supplement |
| Choline | 100–450 mg | Rarely adequate in prenatals |
| Vitamin B12 | 2.6 mcg+ | Especially important for vegans/vegetarians |

### Important Caveats
- No standard federal definition exists for what a "prenatal supplement" must contain [2].
- Product formulations vary widely. A 2023 analysis found only 1 of 69 products contained optimal target doses for all six key nutrients [2].
- Gummy formulations often lack iron and calcium [2].
- Look for third-party testing (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab) [2].
- AVOID supplements with high-dose preformed vitamin A (>3,000 mcg RAE) — teratogenic [2].
- 2023 US GAO report: 11 of 12 over-the-counter prenatals had at least one nutrient deviating from label values [2].

### When to Start
- Ideally: Begin prenatal supplements 1–3 months before conception (for folate NTD protection) [2][8].
- If pregnancy is unplanned: Start as soon as pregnancy is confirmed.

## 5. Foods to Avoid

### 5.1 High-Risk Foods

| Food | Risk | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| Soft mould-ripened cheeses (brie, camembert) | Listeria → miscarriage, stillbirth | Avoid unless cooked until steaming hot |
| Soft blue cheeses (gorgonzola, roquefort) | Listeria | Avoid unless cooked until steaming hot |
| Unpasteurised milk/dairy | Listeria, E. coli | Avoid |
| Raw/undercooked meat | Toxoplasmosis → miscarriage, brain damage | Cook thoroughly; no pink meat |
| Pâté (all types, including vegetarian) | Listeria | Avoid |
| Liver and liver products | Excessive vitamin A (teratogenic) | Avoid |
| Raw/undercooked eggs (non-Lion-stamped) | Salmonella | Cook thoroughly |
| Cold-smoked/cured fish (smoked salmon, gravlax) | Listeria | Avoid unless cooked hot |
| Raw shellfish | Bacteria, viruses, toxins | Avoid |
| Shark, swordfish, marlin | High mercury → neurotoxic | Avoid completely |

Source: NHS [5], confirmed by ACOG [1] and British Nutrition Foundation [15].

### 5.2 Foods to Limit

| Food | Limit | Reason |
|------|-------|--------|
| Tuna | ≤4 medium cans or 2 steaks/week | Mercury [5] |
| Oily fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) | ≤2 portions/week | Pollutants (dioxins, PCBs) [5][15] |
| Caffeine | ≤200 mg/day | Miscarriage, low birth weight risk [5][12] |
| Cured meats (salami, chorizo, prosciutto) | Cook thoroughly or limit | Toxoplasmosis risk [5] |

### 5.3 Caffeine

- **Limit: ≤200 mg/day** (ACOG [1], NHS [5])
- Reference amounts:
  - 1 mug instant coffee = ~100 mg
  - 1 mug filter coffee = ~140 mg
  - 1 mug tea = ~75 mg
  - 1 can cola = ~40 mg
  - 1 energy drink (250ml) = ~80 mg
- **Evidence:** Meta-analysis (2023) [12]: Dose-response relationship with pregnancy loss. Low intake (50–149 mg/d): no significant risk (RR 1.02). High intake (350–699 mg/d): RR 1.40 (95% CI: 1.16–1.68). Very high (≥700 mg/d): RR 1.72 (95% CI: 1.40–2.13).
- Caffeine also associated with low birth weight in dose-response manner [13].

### 5.4 Alcohol

- **Recommendation: Complete avoidance** (ACOG [1], NHS [5], WHO [3]).
- No safe level of alcohol consumption during pregnancy has been established.
- Alcohol crosses the placenta; fetal liver cannot metabolize it effectively.
- Risks: Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD), miscarriage, stillbirth, preterm birth, low birth weight, neurodevelopmental problems.

## 6. Dietary Patterns and Pregnancy Outcomes

### Mediterranean Diet

The Mediterranean diet — rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, olive oil, nuts, legumes, and fish — has emerging evidence for pregnancy benefits:

- **GDM prevention:** Systematic review of RCTs (2024) [9] suggests Mediterranean diet introduced in pregnancy may reduce GDM incidence. Observational meta-analysis (Nature Nutrition & Diabetes, 2024) [10] confirms association between higher MD adherence and lower GDM risk.
- **Broader perinatal outcomes:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (2023) [11] of RCTs and cohort studies shows association with improved perinatal outcomes including reduced preeclampsia and excessive gestational weight gain.
- **Mechanism (inference):** High fiber, healthy fats, antioxidants, and anti-inflammatory properties likely improve glycemic control and reduce oxidative stress.

### General Principles of a Healthy Pregnancy Diet

- **Variety:** No single food provides all needed nutrients [1][3].
- **Whole foods over processed:** Minimize ultra-processed foods, added sugars, and excess sodium.
- **Hydration:** Adequate water intake (approximately 8–12 cups/day).
- **Regular meals:** Small, frequent meals may help with nausea (first trimester) and heartburn (third trimester).

## 7. Special Considerations

### Vegetarian and Vegan Diets
Can be nutritionally adequate with planning but require attention to [2]:
- **Vitamin B12** (supplementation essential — no plant sources)
- **Iron** (plant sources have lower bioavailability; pair with vitamin C)
- **DHA** (algae-based supplements needed)
- **Calcium** (fortified plant milks, tofu)
- **Zinc** (legumes, seeds; absorption lower from plant sources)
- **Iodine** (if not using iodized salt)
- **Protein** (combine plant protein sources)

### Morning Sickness
- Eat small, frequent meals; avoid empty stomach.
- Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) at 10–25 mg three times daily may help reduce nausea [8].
- Ginger may provide mild relief [2].
- Avoid trigger foods (strong smells, fatty/spicy foods).

### Gestational Diabetes
- If diagnosed: dietary management is first-line treatment.
- Focus on complex carbohydrates, fiber, and protein to manage blood sugar.
- Work with dietitian for individualized meal planning.
- Mediterranean diet principles may help glycemic control [9][10].

## 8. Risks of Key Deficiencies

| Deficiency | Maternal Risk | Fetal/Infant Risk |
|------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Folate | Megaloblastic anemia | Neural tube defects, orofacial clefts [2][8] |
| Iron | Anemia (27.5% in 3rd trimester in US), fatigue | Preterm birth, low birth weight [2][8] |
| Vitamin D | Preeclampsia risk, bone loss | Preterm birth, rickets, impaired growth [2][8] |
| Calcium | Bone loss, preeclampsia (if intake <600 mg/d) | Impaired bone mineralization [2] |
| Iodine | Goiter, hypothyroidism | Cretinism, intellectual disability [2] |
| Choline | Unknown maternal effects | Impaired brain development, NTDs [2][8] |
| DHA | Possible depression risk | Suboptimal brain/eye development, preterm birth [8] |
| Vitamin B12 | Megaloblastic anemia, neurological symptoms | NTDs, low birth weight, neurodevelopmental issues [2][8] |
| Vitamin A (excess) | — | Birth defects (teratogenic if >3,000 mcg preformed) [2] |

## 9. Evidence Quality and Limitations

### Strengths
- Strong guideline convergence across countries on core recommendations
- RDA/AI values based on extensive DRI committee review (National Academies) [2]
- Multiple meta-analyses supporting key nutrients: folate→NTDs, iron→anemia, calcium→preeclampsia [2]
- Food safety guidance based on microbiological evidence [5]

### Limitations
- Optimal doses for some nutrients (vitamin D, choline, DHA) still debated [8]
- Most supplement RCTs done in high-income countries
- Dietary pattern research (Mediterranean) is still emerging for pregnancy-specific endpoints [9][10][11]
- Individual variation in nutrient needs is large
- Compliance with recommendations is often poor

## Open Questions

1. Should the vitamin D RDA for pregnancy be increased above 600 IU? (optimization studies suggest 1,000–4,000 IU) [8]
2. What is the optimal choline intake for fetal neurodevelopment?
3. Can Mediterranean diet interventions meaningfully reduce GDM in clinical practice? [9]
4. What is the precise dose-response for caffeine and pregnancy loss at low-moderate intakes? [12]
5. Should DHA supplementation be universal, or targeted to women with low fish intake?
6. How can prenatal supplement quality and standardization be improved? [2]
